# The Thing That $till Counts Later

## A Structural Derivation of Money Before Its Functions

This repository contains the source text for **The Thing That $till Counts Later: A Structural Derivation of Money Before Its Functions**.

This paper offers a structural derivation of money before its familiar textbook functions. It does not begin with medium of exchange, unit of account, store of value, means of payment, legal tender, bank liability, ledger balance, commodity, token, trust, or payment infrastructure. Those surfaces matter, but they do not by themselves identify the money-object.

## Why this matters

Money is often defined by what it does, what it looks like, who issues it, how it is recorded, or how it is recognized. Each route touches something real. But when any one surface is treated as the whole object, money becomes unstable.

This paper asks a prior question:

**What must be structurally true before anything can function as money?**

## Core claim

Money is carried can-settle in amount-bearing form.

More fully, money is the derivative substrate-level carrier by which a money-form bears portable, amount-bearing, temporally window-stable, uptake-stabilized settlement capacity across encounters, parties, and time, without requiring users to traverse the originating relation for ordinary settlement use.

## What this is not

This paper does not offer a full theory of monetary economics, banking, currency regimes, monetary policy, central banking, financial stability, or the academic literature on money.

It derives the money-object before those downstream questions begin.

## How it was done

The paper begins from non-guarantee: economic consequence does not automatically persist, transfer, remain identifiable, remain amount-stable, or remain acceptable later. Money appears where settlement capacity must travel across encounters without requiring every later user to reconstruct the full origin story of the carrier.

## Where next

This artifact sits downstream of the Structural Flow economics foundation and alongside other economics-object artifacts. It supports later work on monetary systems, credit money, banking layers, payment rails, liquidity, legal tender, token systems, and monetary failure.
